Complete Guide to KYC in Mexico (2024)

Last updated: November 2024 • 15 min read

Know Your Customer (KYC) in Mexico refers to the regulatory requirements for businesses to verify the identity of their customers before establishing a business relationship. This guide covers everything you need to know about Mexican KYC requirements, required documents, and compliance best practices.

Key Takeaway

Mexican KYC requires verifying customer identity through official documents like the SAT Constancia (tax certificate), Acta Constitutiva (for companies), INE (national ID), and proof of address. All financial institutions and many businesses must comply with AML regulations enforced by the UIF (Financial Intelligence Unit).

What is KYC in Mexico?

KYC (Know Your Customer) is the process of verifying a customer's identity and assessing potential risks before doing business with them. In Mexico, KYC requirements are mandated by:

  • LFPIORPI - Federal Law for Prevention and Identification of Operations with Illicit Resources
  • CNBV - National Banking and Securities Commission regulations
  • SAT - Tax Administration Service requirements
  • UIF - Financial Intelligence Unit reporting requirements

Required Documents for Business KYC in Mexico

When onboarding a Mexican business customer, you typically need to collect and verify these documents:

1. SAT Constancia de Situación Fiscal

The SAT Constancia is the most important document for business verification. It's an official certificate from Mexico's tax authority that confirms:

  • RFC (Tax ID number) - unique identifier for the business
  • Legal business name
  • Fiscal address (official registered address)
  • Tax regime and status
  • Economic activities (CFDI codes)
  • Tax obligations

2. Acta Constitutiva

The incorporation deed (Acta Constitutiva) establishes the legal existence of the company and provides:

  • Company identity and legal name
  • Shareholders and ownership structure
  • Legal representatives and their powers
  • Corporate purpose and activities
  • Registered capital
  • Notary information and registration details

3. INE/IFE of Legal Representatives

The INE (Instituto Nacional Electoral) voter ID is Mexico's primary identification document. For business KYC, you need INE copies of:

  • Legal representatives
  • Authorized signatories
  • Beneficial owners (if applicable)

4. Proof of Address

Recent utility bills (no more than 3 months old) serve as proof of address:

  • CFE - Electricity bill (most common)
  • Telmex - Phone/internet bill
  • Water bill
  • Bank statement showing address

5. Bank Statements

Recent bank statements provide additional verification and financial context:

  • Account holder name (must match other documents)
  • Account address
  • Transaction history
  • Average balances

RFC Validation

The RFC (Registro Federal de Contribuyentes) is Mexico's tax identification number and is critical for KYC. A valid RFC:

  • For individuals: 13 characters (4 letters + 6 digits + 3 alphanumeric)
  • For companies: 12 characters (3 letters + 6 digits + 3 alphanumeric)
  • Contains encoded information about the entity's name and founding date

RFC Format Example

Company: ABC123456XY9
Individual: GAPA850101ABC

The first characters come from the name, followed by the date of birth/founding, and a verification code.

Mexican AML Regulations

Mexico's Anti-Money Laundering framework requires "vulnerable activities" (actividades vulnerables) to implement KYC procedures:

Key AML Requirements

  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) - Identify and verify all customers
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) - Additional scrutiny for high-risk customers
  • Beneficial Ownership - Identify ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs)
  • Transaction Monitoring - Monitor for suspicious patterns
  • Reporting - File reports with UIF for relevant transactions
  • Record Keeping - Maintain records for minimum 5 years

KYC Validation Best Practices

  1. Cross-reference documents - Verify that names, addresses, and RFC numbers match across all documents
  2. Check document recency - Proof of address should be no more than 3 months old
  3. Validate RFC with SAT - Confirm RFC is active and matches SAT records
  4. Verify representative powers - Ensure legal representatives have proper authority per Acta
  5. Address discrepancy handling - Understand fiscal vs. operational address differences
  6. Maintain audit trail - Document all verification steps and decisions

Common KYC Challenges in Mexico

  • Document variations - Mexican documents come in many formats and layouts
  • Address inconsistencies - Fiscal and operational addresses often differ
  • Manual processing - Traditional KYC requires significant manual effort
  • RFC validation complexity - Checking RFC status requires SAT system access
  • Multi-document correlation - Matching data across multiple documents is error-prone

Summary

KYC in Mexico requires collecting and verifying multiple documents to establish customer identity and comply with AML regulations. The core documents are:

  1. SAT Constancia - Tax status and RFC verification
  2. Acta Constitutiva - Legal company structure
  3. INE - Representative identity
  4. Proof of Address - CFE or Telmex bills
  5. Bank Statements - Financial verification
